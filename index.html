Analysis and discussion

The discovery of synthetic fuel production remains to be not exactly known with some speculation that it was first sited in the United States and others saying that a biodiesel was used in the Czech Republic for oil lamps before that. It was in Germany that the first the publicise the production of synthetic fuel. Friedrich Bergius, a German scientist, played a vital role in advancing the conversion of coal into synthetic fuel through the Bergius process. (Friedrich Bergius (2023) Encyclopædia Britannica. Available at: https://www.britannica.com/biography/Friedrich-Bergius (Accessed: 30 October 2023). Simultaneously, another noteworthy project was gaining ground, the Fischer-Tropsch synthesis (FTS), developed by Franz Fischer and Hans Tropsch. (Dry, M. (2004) ‘page 1-32’, in Fischer-Tropsch technology. Amsterdam: Elsevier, pp. 1–32.) The Fischer process stood as a foundation layer for the development of this energy source.

Despite these pioneering efforts, the awareness of the development of synthetic fuels remained relatively quiet due to inefficiencies and high costs, especially when compared to the well-known and well-used diesel and petroleum. Germany took a significant interest in synthetic fuels to reduce its dependence on petroleum imports. (Dry, M. (2004) ‘page 1-32’, in Fischer-Tropsch technology. Amsterdam: Elsevier, pp. 1–32.) During 1930s, the Nazi party focused its attention on synthetic fuels, not only as an economic standpoint but also in preparation for outrunning any enemies in the war. In a controversial headline, synthetic fuel production was banned in Germany in 1945 (THE CONFERENCE OF BERLIN (1945) U.S. Department of State. Available at: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1945Berlinv02/d848 (Accessed: 05 November 2023)). This ban led to the dismantling and closure of FTS plants, marking a sudden halt to the industry.

However, this was not the end for synthetic fuels. Already in the 1930s, South African companies displayed interest in the subject, investing in the construction of FTS plants in South Africa. (Hilsenrath, P. (1989) The development of synthetic fuels in South Africa - JSTOR, jstor. Available at: https://www.jstor.org/stable/24807938 (Accessed: 03 November 2023)) After the war, the United States reignited its archaic enthusiasm for synthetic fuels.

A milestone for the United States arrived on April 5th, 1944, when Congress approved the Synthetic Fuels Act. This legislation allocated $20 million resulting in the establishment of new laboratories and plant focused on advancing synthetic fuels. (Vietor, R.H.K. (1980) The Synthetic Liquid Fuels Program: Energy politics in the ... - JSTOR, jstor. Available at: https://www.jstor.org/stable/3114274 (Accessed: 03 November 2023). Modern synthetic fuels, more commonly known as e-fuels, offering a new hope. The prestigious automobile company Porsche has been discreetly testing and developing e-fuels. This innovative fuel could potentially convert any combustion engines carbon dioxide-free with only the need for minor tweaks to the tuning of the car. In mid-2022, a plant in Chile began production of this new fuel. The e-fuel production process involves water and carbon dioxide. Hydrogen is generated via electrolysis, employing a direct current to split water molecules. The hydrogen is collected at the cathode, while the anode attracts carbon dioxide. This process, referred to as methanol synthesis, yields e-methanol, which is subsequently transformed into synthetic petrol through another process known as MTG synthesis. (Efuels. Available at: https://media.porsche.com/mediakit/innovation-sustainability-performance/en/innovation-sustainability-performance/synthetic-fuels (Accessed: 03 November 2023).)

Porsche was determined to be producing around 130,000 litre of e-fuel a year in their new Chile production plant by the end of 2022. They showcased their innovation by fuelling all their pre-1966 911 cars at the 2023 Goodwood Revival Festival. (Jupp, E.
